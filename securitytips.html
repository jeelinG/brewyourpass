6 Tips to Improve Password Security

01
Create Strong, Unique Passwords
  • Length matters: Aim for passwords that are at least 12 characters long; 14 or more is even better. Longer passwords are significantly harder to crack.
  • Mix it up: Use a combination of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols.
  • Avoid common words and phrases: Don't use dictionary words, names, dates of birth, pet names, or other easily guessable information.
  • Unique for each account: Use a different password for every website and application. If one account is compromised, the others will remain secure.
  • Consider passphrases: These are memorable sequences of unrelated words (e.g., "blue table jumping elephant"). They are long and relatively easy to remember but difficult to guess.

02
Use a Password Manager
  • Secure storage: They store your passwords in an encrypted vault, so you only need to remember one strong master password.
  • Identify weak passwords: Many password managers can analyze your existing passwords and identify weak or reused ones.

03
Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
  • Add an extra layer of security: MFA requires a second verification step in addition to your password, such as a one-time code from an app, a fingerprint, or a security key.
  • Highly recommended: Enable MFA whenever it's offered, especially for important accounts like email, banking, and social media.

04
Be Careful Where You Enter Passwords
  • Use trusted networks: Avoid entering sensitive information, including passwords, on public Wi-Fi networks.
  • Check for secure connections: Ensure that the website you are on has "HTTPS" in the address bar and a padlock icon, indicating an encrypted connection.
  • Beware of phishing: Be cautious of emails, messages, or calls asking for your password. Legitimate services will never ask for your password in this way. Always access websites directly through bookmarks or by typing the address yourself.

05
Manage and Maintain Your Passwords
  • Don't share passwords: Never tell anyone your passwords, not even friends or family.
  • Avoid writing them down: If you must write them down, keep them in a secure place away from the device they protect, or consider writing down hints instead of the actual password.
  • Change compromised passwords immediately: If you suspect an account has been compromised, change the password immediately.
  • Consider periodic password updates: While not as critical as using strong, unique passwords, changing them occasionally can add another layer of security. However, prioritize creating strong and unique passwords in the first place.
  • Don't reuse old passwords: When changing passwords, don't use any that you have used before.
  • Be wary of "remember me" options: Use these cautiously, especially on shared devices.

06
Educate Yourself and Others
  • Stay informed: Keep up-to-date on the latest password security threats and best practices.
  • Educate family and friends: Share these tips with others to help them improve their online security.
